Connected Spaces

Curated by Angela Bottazzi

Curatorial Statement

Welcome to "Connected Spaces," this is an exhibition that explores how artists use digital networks to be collaborative and share creative experiences. I have brought together works from the late 20th century, the exhibition highlights how early net artists reimagined the internet not just as a tool, but as a space for connection and collective authorship to happen. At the center is The World in 24 Hours (1982), which showd a global artistic event that linked participants across cities through telecommunications. This emphasized real-time exchange and collaboration. This idea is expanded in later works like Epithelia (1999), which invites users to participate to generate a constantly evolving visual structure, and skinonskinonskin (1999), explores intimacy and interaction through networked digital interfaces. Together, these works demonstrate how net art transforms audiences into participants and highlights the internet as a space for distributed creativity, shared authorship, and human connection across distance.

The World in 24 Hours (1982) – Robert Adrian

This piece connected artists across multiple cities through telecommunications networks, creating a global, time based collaborative artwork. This piece helped demonstrate how artistic exchange could happen in real time across distances, making the network itself part of the creative process.

Epithelia (1999) – Marek Walczak & Martin Wattenberg

This interactive web based artwork allows users to contribute to a growing visual system made up of browser windows. Each participant’s input becomes part of a larger, evolving digital structure, emphasizing collaboration and collective creation.

skinonskinonskin (1999) – Entropy8Zuper! (Auriea Harvey & Michaël Samyn)

This piece explores intimacy and connection through interactive digital environments, where users engage with layered visuals and interfaces. It highlights how networks can create emotional and sensory experiences, not just technical ones.
